Syn-Bio Genomics

The importance of biotechnological scaffolds has been proved widely in a broad range of applications, from tissue regeneration1 to the enhancement of bioproduction pathways2. Exploiting the self-assembly ability of the rotavirus middle capsid protein VP6 into different structures such as spheres and nanotubes, we propose a synthetic biological system to generate a multi-purpose nanotechnological scaffold. The advantage of the aforementioned scaffold lies in its self-assemblage capacity, and in its potential of being readily modified for specific purposes.

The Rotascaffold Project

Overview

Web Design

Rotavirus is a double stranded RNA virus covered by a triple-layered icosahedral capsid. The outermost layer is composed of viral proteins VP4 and VP7, the middle layer of VP6 and the inner capsid of VP23. VP6 is capable of self assembling into different structures depending on the pH and the ionic strength of its medium4. Furthermore, these structures can be functionalized with metals such as gold, silver or paladium5.

The interactions between the different proteins of the rotavirus capsid and VP6 have been previously described6,7,8. Briefly, VP7 interacts with VP6 through 30 amino acids in its N-terminal tail6. VP2 adopts a three dimensional structure that allows interaction with residues 64-72 of VP67. In addition, VP4 forms a spike-like structure that interacts with VP6, and has been described as the “viral attachment protein”8. These biological interactions and the aforestated VP6 nanostructures  could be modified and exploited in the context of synthetic biology.

Synthetic biology can be broadly defined as an emerging field of research that combines biological and engineering approaches in the design, development and study of new biological systems with novel properties and applications. In this project, we propose the development of an expression system aimed to generate a multifunctional self-assembling scaffold with a synthetic biology focus. This scaffold will consist of nanostructures formed by VP6 subunits, bound together by a set of adaptor peptides. In addition, another set of adaptor peptides can be used to attach any enzyme or metabolic pathway of interest to the scaffold. As a proof of concept, the production of the violacein metabolic pathway using this scaffold will be assessed and compared with the traditional overexpression models. As an application of this novel tool, we will evaluate the ability for our system to boost the efficiency of the styrene enzymatic degradation pathway. This polluting compound is considered a perilous chemical for human beings13.

Development of the adaptor peptides (Linkers)

The constructions of the adaptor peptides will take advantage of the natural interactions of VP6 with VP7 and VP4. Two types of adaptor peptides will be constructed: (1) those that bind VP6 nanostructures together forming the base scaffold, and (2) those that join the base scaffold with the enzymes from the pathway of interest. These peptides will be translationally fused with the forenamed enzymes.

VP6 expression in Escherichia coli

In order to accomplish the expression of self-assemblable and exportable VP6 in E. coli, and at the same time to avoid an excessive metabolic load and the production of inclusion bodies, we will elaborate and test a series of constructions varying the strength of both the promoters and ribosome binding sites (RBS) with the addition of an exportation tag to the viral protein VP6. Therefore, we look for controlled and stable transcription and translation rates and also the facilitated recovery of medium exported VP6. In order to visualize the final VP6 self-assembly in nanostructures, electron microscopy techniques will be used.

Proof of concept: Violacein production

In order to assess the effectivity of our scaffold, we will fuse the adaptors with the violacein pathway enzymes12 and coexpress both the scaffold and the adaptor-enzyme fusions in our E. coli system. The efficiency improvement will be measured through the absorbency quantification of the produced pigment compared against the production with the usual enzyme overexpression methods as a control.  We expect our system to lead to a higher yield than the control due to the local increase in the enzymatic concentration and a  substrate-channeling-like activity of the scaffold.

The Main Problem

John Doe

Controlling the spatial organization of sequential metabolic enzymes may provide a general strategy for increasing the production of valuable compounds9.  There are several advantages in organizing a local concentration of enzymes into a scaffold which include: (1) the prevention of the loss of intermediates by diffusion, (2) forestall entrance of intermediates into competing metabolic pathways and (3) the reduction of the metabolic load to the cell, as enzymes don’t need to be over-expressed to achieve optimal yields2,10. Our system exploits the benefits of an organizing scaffold and profits from the self-assembling capabilities of VP6 into different structures like nanotubes or nanospheres4. Finally, our multifunctional scaffold system will enable the addition of enzymes from diverse pathways through the translational fusion of small adaptor peptides to the enzymes of interest.

John Doe

We propose two metabolic pathways as a direct application of our system. In both pathways, we hypothesize that the organization of enzymes in our scaffold will achieve better efficiency than conventional overexpression methods. The first pathway involves the production of violacein from L-tryptophan. Violacein is a violet pigment easily observable and quantifiable11.  Furthermore, violacein and its derivatives are of interest in the development of antibacterial, antitrypanosomal, antiulcer and anticancer drugs11. This pathway consists of five enzymes and it has been tested in E. coli previously12.  The second pathway to be assessed is the enzymatic degradation of styrene into phenylacetic acid. Styrene is used in large quantities by the chemical industry13. Airborne emissions of styrene, even at low concentrations, constitute a major pollution problem due the toxic and carcinogenic effects of the compound13. The gene cluster that confers the ability to degrade styrene comes from Pseudomonas sp. strain Y2 and consists of four catabolic and two regulatory genes14. This cluster has been expressed in E. coli, which is  subsequently able to use the styrene as carbon and energy source14.
